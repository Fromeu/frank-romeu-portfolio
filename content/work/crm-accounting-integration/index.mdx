After a deal closed in HubSpot's CRM, sales reps hit a wall. They'd wait for
the finance team to create invoices, with zero visibility on status unless
they directly asked. Finance teams wanted to help reps move faster but
didn't trust them to operate in accounting tools. And for everyone
involved, creating invoices meant re-entering data that already existed in
the CRM — customer details, product information, payment terms — into a
separate system. This was my first project at HubSpot, and every decision I
made on the QuickBooks Online integration would template the framework for
every accounting tool that followed.

## My role

I was the sole product designer, responsible for end-to-end design:
research planning and facilitation, problem definition, information
architecture, user flows, wireframes through high-fidelity designs,
prototyping, usability testing, and ongoing iteration based on continuous
customer feedback. I facilitated collaborative workshops with the team to
drive alignment on problems and solutions, and maintained a regular
advisory group of customers throughout development.

The team: one product manager, two tech leads (front-end and back-end),
six software engineers, one content designer, and one product analyst.

## The problem

### User problem

Once a deal closed in HubSpot, sales reps hit a wall. They'd wait for the
finance team to create invoices, with zero visibility on status unless they
directly asked. Finance teams wanted to help reps move faster but didn't
trust them to operate in accounting tools. And for everyone involved,
creating invoices meant re-entering data that already existed in the CRM —
customer details, product information, payment terms — into a separate
system.

<PullQuote attribution="Customer feedback">
The complaint is always, always spending so much time putting in the same
information over and over again. Shipping address, billing address, then
mailing address...
</PullQuote>

The problem wasn't a single broken step. It was a fractured handoff between
two systems that created delay, redundant work, and an information
asymmetry between the people closing deals and the people processing
payments.

### Business problem

HubSpot positions its CRM as the system of record for customer
relationships. But a CRM that can't tell you whether a customer has paid is
incomplete in a way that matters. Accounting data — invoice status, payment
history, revenue tied to specific deals — is the signal that closes the
loop between sales activity and business outcomes. Without it, HubSpot's
CRM was strong on the front office and blind to the back office.

This project was strategic in a second sense: the QuickBooks Online
integration would serve as the model for an accounting API that external
tools (Xero, NetSuite, FreshBooks, Nubox) could build on. The design
decisions weren't just for one integration — they were for a category.

## Context and constraints

This was my first project at HubSpot. I was new to the company, the SaaS
industry, and the quote-to-cash process, which meant the research phase was
doing double duty as onboarding. That was a constraint and an advantage: I
had no inherited assumptions about how HubSpot "should" handle accounting
data.

The QuickBooks Online integration was the implementation, but the real
deliverable was a reusable framework. Every design decision had to account
for generalized use: what worked for QBO also needed to work for Xero,
NetSuite, and tools that didn't exist yet. I couldn't optimize for any
single accounting tool's quirks without considering the abstraction layer
underneath.

This was also a net-new product category for HubSpot — no existing
integration, no baseline metrics, no historical data to measure improvement
against. We couldn't say "reduce time-to-invoice by X%" because nobody had
measured time-to-invoice in HubSpot before. Success metrics had to be
defined from scratch around adoption signals rather than improvement
metrics.

Invoices involve customer details, product details, and payment details —
all of which needed to match between HubSpot and the accounting tool. Tax
codes for non-US customers, currency conversions, and differing sources of
truth between systems created design challenges that couldn't be
simplified away.

## Process

Before committing to any feature direction, the PM and I split research
responsibilities. She sourced, recruited, and scheduled 15 interviews with
business owners, accountants, and sales reps across the quote-to-cash
chain. I developed the research plan and facilitated the discussions,
asking how customers currently measured growth in the CRM, where the
greatest friction in their quote-to-cash process was, who felt that
friction most acutely, and where the process broke down.

While interviews were running, I built an iterative diagram mapping the
invoice lifecycle — plotting touchpoints and handoffs between sales reps,
customers, and finance teams. This diagram became a working artifact that
evolved throughout the project, serving as both a sense-making tool for me
and an alignment artifact for the team.

Synthesis laid customer quotes against our research questions, identified
themes, and plotted friction points against the lifecycle diagram. The core
problems that emerged: sales reps waiting on finance to create invoices,
zero visibility on invoice status without bothering finance, finance
wanting to help reps but not trusting them in accounting tools,
time-consuming redundant data entry, and no way to track completion from
closed deal to paid invoice.

I facilitated collaborative workshops with the full team: summarize
research findings, align on the problems we'd solve, review the primary
persona (sales rep — the clearest beneficiary from the research), generate
approaches, and plot ideas on an effort-versus-impact matrix for
prioritization. The workshops produced a set of user requirements: the
ability to create invoices from a deal record, match customers and
products between systems, send invoices to the external tool, monitor
invoice status from a deal record, and create automated workflows based on
invoice data.

I broke down the categories of information that flow through an invoice —
customer details, product details, payment details — which informed the
data-matching requirements between HubSpot and QuickBooks, then diagrammed
user flows for invoice creation, including matching logic for products and
customers, matching HubSpot quotes to invoice drafts, and the various
creation paths between systems.

Solution design moved through multiple rounds of sketching, wireframing,
and high-fidelity work — but it didn't follow a clean handoff from design
to build. We maintained regular communication with a customer advisory
group throughout development, which let us iterate while the product was
being built, not just before. That recurring feedback loop produced
significant changes over time: reworking the side-panel invoice creation
into a full-screen experience, adding the ability to import existing
invoices, enabling invoice creation directly from quote data, adding
contact and invoice syncing, and building features for a QuickBooks Online
Advanced premium partnership.

Several problems resisted clean solutions. Tax code selection for non-US
customers was complex because tax structures vary dramatically across
jurisdictions and accounting tools handle them differently. Currency
conversions raised source-of-truth questions — when HubSpot and the
accounting tool disagreed on exchange rates, which one won? Matching unique
quote data to invoice drafts required careful mapping logic. Import speeds
from some accounting tools were painfully slow, requiring loading-state
design that acknowledged the wait rather than hiding it. These weren't
edge cases — they were the daily reality for businesses operating across
borders or with complex product catalogs, and the design had to handle
them without overwhelming users who had simpler setups.

## Key decisions

### 1. Full-screen experience over side panel

**What was decided:** Rework the original side-panel invoice creation flow
into a full-screen experience.

**Alternatives considered:** Keep the side panel and simplify the invoice
creation flow instead — fewer fields, fewer options.

**Why this direction:** Customer feedback during iterative testing made it
clear that creating an invoice isn't a lightweight action — it involves
matching customers, selecting products, configuring tax codes, and
reviewing payment terms. The side panel cramped the content and made the
task feel like a quick edit when it was actually a structured workflow.

**What it cost:** Breaking the pattern of side-panel actions on deal
records, which required alignment with other design teams across HubSpot.

### 2. Quote-to-invoice pre-fill

**What was decided:** Let invoice drafts pre-fill from existing HubSpot
quote data, editable before sending.

**Alternatives considered:** Treat quotes and invoices as fully
independent objects — simpler technically, more robust against data drift
between the two.

**Why this direction:** Reps were already entering deal and product
information into HubSpot quotes. Asking them to re-enter the same data for
an invoice was the exact redundancy customers complained about most.

**What it cost:** A new dependency between the quoting and invoicing
systems that needs ongoing maintenance as both evolve — but the user value
was immediate and addressed the top complaint from research directly.

### 3. Framework-first design discipline

**What was decided:** Design the QBO integration knowing every decision
would become a template for external accounting tools building on the API
— resisting QBO-specific optimizations that wouldn't generalize.

**Alternatives considered:** Optimize fully for QBO first (the largest,
most important integration) and abstract later.

**Why this direction:** Retrofitting abstraction is harder than building
it in from the start, and the accounting extension's credibility depended
on external tools being able to replicate the native experience.

**What it cost:** A longer design process, and some compromises on
QBO-specific polish along the way.

## The solution

The accounting extension gives sales reps the ability to create, monitor,
and manage invoices directly from HubSpot deal records, with data synced to
connected accounting tools.

Nubox — to build equivalent integrations.

## Outcomes

550+ QuickBooks Online installs, 10,000+ total installs across all tools
built on the accounting API, 19,000 invoices created or imported to
HubSpot, and $17M in total revenue on invoices in the platform. These are
adoption metrics, not improvement metrics — we had no baseline to measure
against, a known limitation from the start. They demonstrate that the
product found real usage, but they don't directly measure whether the
quote-to-cash process got faster or whether reps spend less time on
invoice administration.

<PullQuote attribution="NPS respondent, business development coach">
I rarely endorse any CRM because I stay vendor-neutral with my clients, but
this integration is perfect for my small business clients.
</PullQuote>

The integration was featured at HubSpot's annual INBOUND product showcase.
The accounting API enabled external tools to build integrations
replicating the native experience, validating the framework approach. The
customer advisory group stayed engaged throughout the year-and-a-half
development process, providing consistent signal that kept the design
aligned with real usage.

What's still unknown: time from deal-close to paid invoice — the metric we
most wanted but couldn't benchmark — long-term adoption curves per
accounting tool, whether the framework approach produced quality parity
across external integrations or whether QBO maintained an advantage, and
the impact of the premium QBO Advanced partnership features on overall
adoption.

## Reflection

This was my first project at HubSpot, and it shaped how I approach design
work at the company. The biggest lesson was about the dual nature of
framework design: every UX decision had two audiences — the sales rep
using the QBO integration today, and the developer building the Xero
integration next quarter. That tension forced a discipline I wouldn't have
developed on a single-product project.

What worked: the continuous feedback loop with the customer advisory
group. Instead of a research-then-build sequence, we ran research alongside
development, which meant changes like the side-panel-to-full-screen shift
happened while there was still room to make them, not after the pattern
had calcified. The workshop-driven alignment process also worked well for
a cross-functional team that needed to converge on scope quickly.

What didn't work: the measurement story. We set adoption metrics because
we had to measure something, but the metrics we actually cared about —
time-to-invoice, completion rate from closed deal to paid invoice —
weren't measurable because there was no baseline. I'd push harder at the
outset to instrument the existing process, even a rough manual measurement
of how long the deal-to-invoice handoff took, so we'd have something to
compare against post-launch.

The cross-system complexity — tax codes, currencies, matching logic — was
also harder than I initially estimated. I treated those as edge cases
early in the design process, and they turned out to be core flows for a
meaningful portion of users. If I were starting over, I'd front-load the
international and multi-currency scenarios in research rather than
discovering their complexity during iteration.

What I took forward: the instinct to design for the framework, not just
the feature. This project was my introduction to systems-level thinking at
HubSpot, and it directly informed how I've approached every multi-surface
design problem since.
