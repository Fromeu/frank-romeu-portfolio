The CRM record page is HubSpot's most-viewed surface. Over 1.5 million monthly
active users land on it to get a 360-degree view of their contacts, companies,
deals, and tickets. Admins who wanted to customize that experience had to
navigate up to thirty discrete settings scattered across the interface, one
section at a time, with no way to see the full picture.

For years, the product architecture (consisting of separate silos for the
left sidebar, middle column, and right sidebar) directly dictated how our
tooling was constructed. The assumption baked into every settings page was
that admins thought about customization the same way engineers had built it:
one section at a time. Research proved that assumption wrong. Admins think
content-first, not structure-first. That finding became the design principle
that reshaped a new product and eventually became HubSpot's customization
platform.

## My role

Lead Product Designer on the CRM Page Editor team, within the CRM UI
Framework group. I owned the end-to-end user experience design — from
initiating the admin vision proposal through research, design, and delivery
to General Availability (GA).

I had newly transitioned into the CRM group and leadership gave me very
little in terms of a long-term roadmap. I led the problem definition,
facilitated the workshops that aligned the team's goals, ran the research
that validated (and invalidated) our hypotheses, and designed every major
interaction in the builder tool.

I also drove the architectural thinking (e.g. a record abstraction framework,
the card builder system, the programmatic page editor vision) that turned a
single product into a reusable platform for other HubSpot teams.

**Team:** 1 Product Manager, 2 Tech Leads (FE and BE). The team was newly
formed specifically to execute on the vision I'd helped shape. Product group
leadership created it after I'd built the case and secured buy-in.

## The problem

### User problem

HubSpot admins are the gatekeepers of their organization's CRM. They're
responsible for configuring record pages so their sales reps, service
agents, and marketers see the right information at the right time. But the
tools we gave them made that job unnecessarily hard.

Record customization settings were fragmented across seven different entry
points per object type. An admin who wanted to configure a contact record
page had to separately customize the left sidebar, the right sidebar, the
overview tab, the custom tab, data highlights, association tables, and
contact association cards — each through its own settings panel, each with
its own interaction patterns.

From CSAT feedback, three themes dominated. First, clutter: "I'm constantly
hopping between pages," "it's like looking for a needle in a haystack."
Seventeen percent of feedback cited difficulty finding what's customizable.

Second, confusion: "It's not intuitive," "nothing is common sense," "there's
a learning curve." Once admins found the right settings, they struggled to
use them.

Third, inflexibility: "It's too limited," "much more control is needed," "I
should be able to customize my whole screen."

The section-by-section approach also made it impossible for admins to see

sidebar for one team, then cross-reference it against the middle column for
a different view, losing context every time they switched.

### Business problem

HubSpot was pushing upmarket. Customers with 200+ employees made up nearly a
quarter of the company's revenue. These larger organizations needed their
CRM to mirror their business processes. SDRs needed different views from
account executives; different record layouts for sales versus service. If
HubSpot couldn't deliver that level of customization, deals were lost.

The strategic bet on customization was well-founded. Standard tracking
flagged roughly 9% of active paying portals as customized. Post-launch
analysis with more robust signals put the real number at 60% of portals with
customized views.

The customization tooling was directly tied to retention and revenue
expansion.

There was a secondary signal worth noting: customization appeared to happen
early, often before monetization. This suggests it wasn't just a retention
driver but a potential conversion lever. Customers who customize during
trial or free tiers were likely converting at higher rates.

But there was a tension to consider. The business wanted to unlock
customization to win upmarket deals. Admins wanted those tools to be easy.
And reps (the end users of whatever admins built) just wanted record pages
that showed them what mattered without making them think about it.

Serving all three meant the tool couldn't just be powerful. It had to be
opinionated enough to prevent admins from creating worse experiences for
their reps.

## Context and constraints

The record page had a fixed three-column layout composed of a left sidebar,
a middle column, and a right sidebar. Each section had been built and
maintained by different teams with different technical approaches. There
was no shared infrastructure for treating a record page as a single
configurable unit.

Each section's customization settings had been shipped incrementally over
time. Features made it to customers, but it created the fragmentation
problem: six settings per object type across five standard objects meant
thirty distinct places an admin might need to visit. This was technical
debt inherited from years of individual teams solving for their space
without a unifying vision.

There was also legitimate pressure on our timelines. Leadership wanted
something shippable within 2023, with an alpha by end of Q1 and GA by Q4.
The team was small and newly formed, so we had to scope aggressively. This
meant making hard calls about what to include and what to defer, knowing
that every deferral was a customer who'd have to wait.

## Process

### Vision framing (Q3 2022)

Upon newly joining the CRM group, I facilitated a series of workshops with
our team leads across the Records Foundation and Records Sidebars teams. We
explored existing problems sourced from previous research, CSAT data, and
our collective experience working with admin customers.

Through structured ideation and voting, the team converged numerous ideas.
The winning solution was a drag-and-drop visual editor. It was high value
and high complexity, but clearly the thing customers were asking for.

### Research — breaking the mental model (Fall 2022)

With the tentative vision on paper, the next question was whether admins
actually wanted this. More importantly, how did they think about
customizing a record page? Our settings were built section-first. Did
admins think that way?

I co-led a research study with our UX research team. Eight admin
participants, each with super admin permissions, responsible for
configuring their organization's HubSpot accounts. Organizations ranged
from 11 to 1,000 employees; covering a good spread across our target
segments.

The method had three phases. Introductory questions about their context and
customization habits. A collaborative exercise in Figma where participants
built their ideal record page from scratch (blank canvas, card components
to choose from, total freedom in placement). And then concept validation of
our builder prototype.

Three findings reshaped our direction.

**Finding 1: Content-first, not sections-first.** Admins consistently
started by identifying the highest-priority content — "What's the most
important thing I need to see?" — and then placed it wherever it felt
right. They didn't think in terms of "I'll configure the left sidebar, then
the middle, then the right."

This directly invalidated our existing architecture, which drove
customization through individual section settings. If we'd built the
unified tool with a sections-first mental model, we'd have recreated the
same fragmentation in a fancier wrapper.

**Finding 2: No consistent spatial preferences.** I'd hoped to find
patterns around how admins think of content spatially on the record. For
example, maybe the left sidebar was always for static info, the middle for
action, the right for reference. What we found was that it depends entirely
on the company, the industry, the role, and the object type.

The one consistent theme was relevance: every admin organized content by
how relevant it was to the person using the record, not by any inherent
property of the section itself. This meant we couldn't prescribe section
purposes. We had to let admins decide.

**Finding 3: They want it now.** After walking through the concept
prototype, seven of eight participants expressed a strong desire to use
this tool. One customer's response became our shorthand: "Yeah, no, I want
it, I want it now." The lone exception said he wouldn't use it unless his
team asked for it. It communicated to us that discovery and awareness would
be critical to adoption.

The content-first insight forced a genuine rethinking of the builder's
information architecture. Instead of organizing around sections, we needed
to make it easy to find and add content first, then let admins place it
freely.

### Architecture and abstraction (Q4 2022 – Q1 2023)

Before jumping to high-fidelity designs, I needed to codify how the record
page actually works as a system. Too many conversations were getting
tangled because we didn't have shared language for the components involved.

I created the record abstraction; a framework that broke the page into
nested container types. At the top level, a page container (subtype: deal,
contact, etc.). Within that, section containers (left sidebar, middle
column, right sidebar). Within sections, tab containers. Within tabs, card
containers. And within cards, component containers that housed the
individual UI elements like property fields or data wells.

This exercise served two purposes: it gave engineering a conceptual model
for the data structure, and it gave me a framework for thinking about which
levels of the hierarchy should support conditionality.

### Design and iteration (Q1 – Q3 2023)

Design happened across several parallel workstreams.

**Settings page redesign.** The immediate goal was reducing seven entry
points to three (left sidebar, middle column, right sidebar), with a path
to eventually consolidating into one. I redesigned the manager's panel to
use a unified table view where all configurations (default + team views)
lived in a single list with metadata like last updated and assigned teams.
This started training customers on the pattern they'd eventually see at the
full page level.

<Figure
  src="/work/crm-page-editor/settings-redesign.jpg"
  alt="Redesigned settings page: unified table view of record customization configurations with metadata"
  caption="The settings redesign: one table replacing seven separate entry points."
/>

**The builder UI.** This was the centerpiece. The builder showed a
low-fidelity representation of the record page with its three sections, and
admins could add cards, move them between tabs, and manage team assignments
without leaving the tool.

A deliberate choice: I used abstract card representations rather than live
renders. Showing actual card content would have been technically expensive
and would have distracted from the configuration task. Instead, each card
showed its name, type, drag handles, and action buttons. For beta, I
expanded this to include collapsible previews to help admins get a sense of
what they were adding with real data.

<Figure
  src="/work/crm-page-editor/unified-builder.png"

**Tab management.** This went through several iterations. The initial
approach used hover states on tabs to reveal edit and delete actions. After
design feedback sessions, I moved to a click-to-reveal dropdown pattern.

The show/hide model for tabs was a deliberate alternative to delete. Hiding
preserved the tab's state so it could be restored, reduced the stakes of
experimentation, and avoided the complexity of managing a separate "saved
tabs" library. It also sidestepped the problem of conveying that tabs don't
persist across views — a concept we weren't ready to support and didn't
want to accidentally imply.

composable "editing modules" (property selection, filtering, sorting) that
could be reused across card types. This modularity was an intentional
investment — it meant other teams building new card types could get editing
UI nearly out of the box.

### Beta, GA, and expanding the surface (Q3 2023 – Spring 2024)

Middle column customization launched at Inbound 2023 as a public beta, with
GA following shortly after in the fall. That release covered the core of
what we'd set out to build with tab management, card configuration, and
team views all within the middle column.

But the middle column was always designed as the first move. The settings
architecture, the full record abstraction, the card builder framework — all
of it was built to scale horizontally across the record page.

**Preview Sidebar Customization (Q1 2024).** The first expansion: applying
the same framework to the preview sidebar that appears when you peek into
any record from any data table in the CRM. We built a preview sidebar
editor with its own settings tab in object settings, team-based views, and
updated user-level customization. Because we'd built the card builder as
composable modules and the manager's panel as a reusable pattern, the
preview sidebar editor reused the same infrastructure without reinventing
the interaction model.

## Key decisions

### 1. Focus on the middle column first

**What was decided:** Scope the 2023 MVP to middle column customization
only (tabs, cards, and team views) while leaving left and right sidebar
customization in their existing settings.

**Alternatives considered:** Full record customization from day one (all
three sections in the builder). Also considered starting with the left
sidebar, since it had the most CSAT complaints.

**Why this direction:** The middle column had the most complexity (tabs,
multiple card types, the activity feed debate) and the most demand.
Building it first allowed us to create the foundation of the builder and
get it right before scaling horizontally.

**What it cost:** Admins still had to use the old settings for sidebars
throughout 2023. The "single point of entry" promise was deferred. Some
customers who were most frustrated with sidebar customization didn't see
relief until 2024.

### 2. Adopt a content-first architecture

**What was decided:** Design the builder so admins start by finding and
adding content (cards), then place it into sections (as opposed to
selecting a section first and customizing it in isolation).

**Alternatives considered:** The sections-first approach we'd already been
using, where each section had its own editor.

**Why this direction:** Research was unambiguous. Every admin in our study
started with "what's the most important thing?" not "which section am I
configuring?" Building sections-first would have recreated the fragmentation
problem inside a new UI. The content-first approach also nudged us toward
the "cards as the atomic unit" mental model, which had long-term platform
implications.

**What it cost:** Engineering complexity increased. A content-first model
required knowing which cards were compatible with which sections and
managing placement rules. It was architecturally harder than isolated
section editors.

### 3. Show/hide tabs instead of delete

**What was decided:** When an admin removes a tab from a view, it's hidden
rather than deleted. Hidden tabs retain their card configuration and can be
restored.

**Alternatives considered:** True deletion with the option to recreate from
scratch. Also a "managed tabs" model where all tabs exist in a library and
admins check/uncheck which ones appear.

**Why this direction:** We weren't supporting saved tabs across views yet,
and a delete-then-recreate flow would have been destructive and
frustrating. Show/hide reduced the stakes by letting admins experiment
freely knowing they could undo if needed. It also subtly reinforced that
tab configuration is deliberate work worth preserving, which aligns with
the admin persona (they plan, collaborate, and iterate on configurations).

**What it cost:** The hidden-tabs UI introduced a new interaction pattern
(a collapsed dropdown for hidden tabs) that added cognitive overhead.

## The solution

The CRM Page Editor is a unified builder tool that consolidates all record
page customization into a single experience.

**What shipped:**

An admin navigates to record customization settings and sees a single table
of page configurations per object type. They click to create or edit a
configuration, and the builder opens.

The builder shows a low-fidelity representation of the full record page.
Admins could add cards from a searchable library, then use drag and drop
for reordering and moving cards between compatible sections.

Custom tabs were managed directly in the builder along with assigned teams
for each view.

Card editing occurs in contextual side panels without leaving the builder.
Each card type surfaces composable editing modules like property selection,
report selection, filtering, etc. This maintained a consistent interaction
pattern and scalable system for card growth.

**Platform evolution:**

What started as a record page tool expanded into something bigger. The card
builder framework (e.g. the composable editing modules, the card SDK, the
schema-driven settings architecture) became a platform that other teams
adopted.

Help Desk integrated the preview sidebar editor to get workspace
customization shipped in weeks instead of months. The CRM Index page team
used the same patterns for their customization settings. I developed the
vision for a Programmatic Page Editor that would let any team in HubSpot
create its own page editor location programmatically, ending the pattern of
every team building bespoke customization tools.

Before this work, every page editor location at HubSpot worked differently.
That was painful to support and created inconsistency for customers. The
Programmatic Page Editor vision put an end to that trajectory. It created a
system where teams get standardized editor infrastructure while retaining
flexibility for their specific needs. Unfortunately, that work was
deprioritized as I transitioned onto a new team mission and priorities
evolved.

## Outcomes

**Quantitative:**

400% increase in unique customizations per month after GA. This was direct
attribution as the Page Editor was the only change to the customization
workflow during this period. 60% of portals were actually customizing their
record pages. 327K accounts have created custom cards (648K total custom
cards). However, 98% have 5 or fewer. The dominant pattern is seemingly
light-touch customization, but this could also be due to a larger share of
our customers being on Free/Starter tiers — smaller businesses with less
sophisticated customization needs.

**Qualitative:**

Customer reception during research was strong. "I want it, I want it now"

## Reflection

The content-first finding was the most consequential moment in this
project. If we'd skipped the research or ran it as a validation exercise
instead of genuine exploration, we'd have built a prettier version of the
same fragmented experience.

The collaborative Figma exercise, where admins built their ideal record
page from scratch, was the method that surfaced the insight. Standard
interview questions wouldn't have caught it because admins would have
described their process using our existing vocabulary (sidebars, tabs,
sections). Giving them a blank canvas forced them to reveal how they
actually think.

The decision to constrain to the middle column first was right, even though
it frustrated customers who needed sidebar improvements. It allowed us to
build the foundation properly and get real feedback before scaling. If we'd
tried to do everything at once, we would have shipped a mediocre version of
all three sections instead of a solid version of one.

Where I'd push harder with a clean slate: I'd fight to include activity
feed relocation in the MVP. Five of eight research participants wanted it,
CSAT data supported it, and it was one of the most visible limitations of
the shipped product. I deferred it because of cross-team complexity, but in
hindsight, the coordination cost was worth paying early.

The evolution into a platform wasn't accidental, but it also wasn't the
original mandate. I made deliberate architectural choices (composable
modules, schema-driven settings, the card SDK) knowing they'd have reusable
value. The platform story emerged from those choices rather than being
planned top-down.

That's a lesson I'll continue to carry: design for the immediate problem,
but make structural decisions that don't foreclose on the bigger picture.
