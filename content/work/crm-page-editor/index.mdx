HubSpot's CRM record page is the product's most-viewed surface — over 1.5 million
monthly active users land on it for a 360-degree view of their contacts, companies,
deals, and tickets. Admins who wanted to customize that experience had to navigate up
to thirty discrete settings scattered across the interface, one section at a time,
with no way to see the full picture. The tooling had been built around how the
product was architected — left sidebar, middle column, right sidebar, each section
its own silo — on the assumption that admins thought about customization the same
way engineers had built it. Research proved that assumption wrong. Admins think
content-first, not structure-first. That finding reshaped a new product, and
eventually became HubSpot's customization platform.

## My role

I was lead product designer on the CRM Page Editor team, within the CRM UI
Framework group, and owned the end-to-end UX — from the initial admin vision
proposal through research, design, and delivery to GA. I led the problem
definition when leadership handed us very little in terms of long-term roadmap,
facilitated the workshops that aligned the team's goals, ran the research that
validated (and invalidated) our early hypotheses, and designed every major
interaction in the builder tool.

I also drove the architectural thinking — the record abstraction framework, the
card builder system, the programmatic page editor vision — that turned a single
product into a reusable platform other HubSpot teams could build on.

The team was small and newly formed specifically to execute on the vision I'd
helped shape: a PM, two tech leads, and a content designer, reporting up through
my design manager. Leadership stood the team up after I'd built the case and
secured buy-in.

## The problem

### User problem

HubSpot admins are the gatekeepers of their organization's CRM. They configure
record pages so sales reps, service agents, and marketers see the right
information at the right time — but the tools we gave them made that job
unnecessarily hard.

Record customization settings were fragmented across seven different entry
points per object type. An admin configuring a contact record page had to
separately customize the left sidebar, the right sidebar, the overview tab, the
custom tab, data highlights, association tables, and contact association cards
— each through its own settings panel, each with its own interaction patterns.

CSAT feedback surfaced three consistent themes: clutter (seventeen percent of
feedback cited difficulty finding what's customizable), confusion (admins found
the right settings, then struggled to use them), and inflexibility (the tools
didn't go far enough).

<PullQuote attribution="CSAT feedback">
It's like looking for a needle in a haystack.
</PullQuote>

The section-by-section approach also made it impossible for admins to see the
bigger picture of what they were building — they'd configure the left sidebar
for one team, then cross-reference it against the middle column for a
different view, losing context every time they switched.

### Business problem

HubSpot was pushing upmarket. Customers with 200+ employees made up nearly a
quarter of the company's revenue, and these larger organizations needed their
CRM to mirror their business processes — different views for SDRs versus
account executives, different record layouts for sales versus service. If
HubSpot couldn't deliver that level of customization, deals walked.

The data was clear: customers who enabled four or more UI customizations had
13 points higher customer success rates, 14 points higher revenue retention,
and 2.5x higher median MRR than customers who hadn't customized at all. This
wasn't a nice-to-have — it was tied directly to retention and expansion
revenue.

But there was a tension. The business wanted to unlock customization to win
upmarket deals. Admins wanted those tools to be easy. Reps — the end users of
whatever admins built — just wanted record pages that showed them what
mattered without making them think about it. Serving all three meant the tool
couldn't just be powerful; it had to be opinionated enough to prevent admins
from creating worse experiences for their reps.

## Context and constraints

The record page had a fixed three-column layout — left sidebar, middle column,
right sidebar — and each section had been built and maintained by different
teams with different technical approaches. There was no shared infrastructure
for treating a record page as a single configurable unit.

Each section's settings had shipped incrementally over time. That got features
out the door, but it created the fragmentation: six settings per object type
across five standard objects meant thirty distinct places an admin might need
to visit — technical debt inherited from years of individual teams solving
their own piece of the puzzle.

A separate platform team was building a generic editor framework we hoped to
build on top of. In theory, yes. In practice, it wasn't ready yet, and we
didn't know how much would come for free versus what we'd need to build
ourselves.

Timeline pressure was real — leadership wanted something shippable within the
year, with an alpha by end of Q1 and GA by Q4, on a small, newly formed team.
And the record page touched multiple teams' domains: moving cards between
sections or introducing new layout options all required buy-in from teams with
their own roadmaps. A meaningful part of the job was building the alignment to
ship it, not just designing the product.

## Process

The work started with a series of workshops across the teams responsible for
the record page's different sections, working through existing problems
sourced from prior research, CSAT data, and the group's collective experience
with admin customers. Through structured ideation and voting, we converged on
five candidate ideas — a visual drag-and-drop editor, a sandbox preview, page
templates, a card library, simplified terminology — and a visual editor won the
prioritization: high complexity, but clearly what customers were asking for.

I synthesized this into a vision proposal: move away from customizing
individual sections in isolation, toward a centralized builder where admins
configure an entire record page as one unit — layout, cards, and views as three
dimensions of a single object. I was explicit that the designs were
placeholders and the direction needed validation, but it gave the team a
shared artifact to react to and a strategy for breaking the work into
milestones.

With the vision on paper, the next question was whether admins actually wanted
this, and more importantly, how they thought about customizing a record page
in the first place. I co-led a research study with eight admin participants
across organizations from 11 to 1,000 employees — introductory questions, a
collaborative Figma exercise where each participant built their ideal record
page from a blank canvas, and concept validation of our builder prototype.

Three findings reshaped the direction. Admins consistently started by
identifying the highest-priority content, not by thinking section-by-section —
which directly invalidated our existing section-first architecture. There was
no consistent spatial preference across companies, industries, or roles; the
one constant was that people organized content by relevance to the person
using the record, not by any inherent property of the section. And the
reception was strong: seven of eight participants expressed real desire to use
the tool.

<PullQuote attribution="Research participant">
Yeah, no, I want it, I want it now.
</PullQuote>

Before high-fidelity design, I codified how the record page actually worked as
a system — a record abstraction that broke the page into nested containers: a
page container, section containers, tab containers, card containers, and
component containers, each with its own version that could change based on
conditions like team assignment or pipeline stage. This gave engineering a
shared data model and gave me a framework for deciding which levels of the
hierarchy needed to support conditionality.

It also surfaced a real design tension: whether views should be tab-first or
view-first. After working through the tradeoffs with engineering, I chose to
keep the existing view-first model for the MVP — consistent with existing
patterns and simpler for customers to learn, at the cost of not scaling as
well to accounts with many tabs.

Design happened across parallel workstreams from there: a redesigned settings
page collapsing seven entry points into three; a three-layer builder UI
(navigation, options, content) showing a low-fidelity record page admins could
add cards to, move between tabs, and manage team assignments on, without
leaving the tool; a tab management pattern that went through several
iterations before landing on click-to-reveal editing with drag handles for
reordering; and, for beta, a move from full-screen card editors to contextual
side panels built from composable editing modules other teams could reuse.

## Key decisions

### 1. Focus on the middle column first

**What was decided:** Scope the initial release to middle column
customization only — tabs, cards, and team views — while leaving left and
right sidebar customization in their existing settings.

**Alternatives considered:** Full record customization from day one. Starting
with the left sidebar instead, since it had the most CSAT complaints.

**Why this direction:** The middle column had the most complexity and the
most demand. Building it first meant getting the core interaction patterns and
the data model right before scaling horizontally, rather than shipping a
mediocre version of all three sections at once.

**What it cost:** Admins kept using the old settings for sidebars through the
first year. The "single point of entry" promise was deferred, and some of the
most frustrated customers didn't see relief until the following year.

### 2. Adopt a content-first architecture

**What was decided:** Design the builder so admins start by finding and
adding content, then place it into sections — rather than picking a section
first and customizing it in isolation.

**Alternatives considered:** Keep the sections-first approach already in
place. A hybrid where sections stayed visible but content was searchable
across all of them.

**Why this direction:** Research was unambiguous — every admin in the study
started with "what's the most important thing?" not "which section am I
configuring?" A sections-first builder would have recreated the fragmentation
problem inside a new coat of paint.

**What it cost:** More engineering complexity. A content-first model meant
knowing which cards were compatible with which sections, managing placement
rules, and handling edge cases when cards moved between sections with
different constraints.

### 3. Show/hide tabs instead of delete

**What was decided:** Removing a tab hides it rather than deleting it.
Hidden tabs retain their configuration and can be restored.

**Alternatives considered:** True deletion with recreate-from-scratch. A
"managed tabs" library model where admins check or uncheck which tabs appear.

**Why this direction:** We weren't supporting saved tabs across views yet,
and delete-then-recreate would have been destructive. Show/hide let admins
experiment freely, knowing they could undo.

**What it cost:** The hidden-tabs pattern introduced its own interaction — a
collapsed dropdown for hidden tabs — that added cognitive overhead. Admins
with many tabs sometimes found the line between "hidden" and "not created yet"
unclear.

### 4. Side panel card editing over full-screen builders

**What was decided:** For beta, card editing moved from full-screen
dedicated builders into side panels within the page editor, using composable
editing modules.

**Alternatives considered:** Keep the existing full-screen card editors, just
launched from the builder. Inline editing directly on the card representations.

**Why this direction:** Full-screen editors broke context — admins lost sight
of the page they were building every time they edited a card. Composable
modules (property selection, filtering, sorting as standalone components)
also created a framework other teams could adopt, which is where the product
started becoming a platform.

**What it cost:** Side panels have less room than full-screen editors.
Complex card types with many settings felt cramped, and we had to be
disciplined about which modules to include.

## The solution

The CRM Page Editor is a unified builder that consolidates record page
customization into a single experience. An admin navigates to record
customization settings and sees a single table of configurations per object
type — replacing the previous seven-link maze — and opens the builder to edit
one.

Card editing happens in contextual side panels without leaving the builder.
Each card type surfaces composable editing modules — property selection,
column configuration, filtering — that keep a consistent interaction pattern
regardless of the card.

What we cut: live card rendering in the builder (abstract representations
instead), layouts beyond three columns, template sharing between admins, and
activity feed relocation, which needed cross-team coordination that couldn't
be scoped into the first year despite being one of the most-requested changes
in research.

What started as a record page tool expanded well beyond it. The card builder
framework — the composable editing modules, the card SDK, the schema-driven
settings architecture — became a platform other teams adopted: a preview
sidebar editor reused the same infrastructure for a hover-preview surface,
another team integrated the framework to ship their own customization in
weeks instead of months, and the CRM Index page team, which I later led,
adopted the same patterns for its own settings. I developed the vision for a
programmatic page editor that would let any team at HubSpot create its own
page editor location on shared infrastructure, ending a long pattern of every
team building bespoke customization tools from scratch.

## Outcomes

Unique customizations per month increased 400% after GA — the only change to
the customization workflow during that period. Customers with four or more UI
customizations saw 13 points higher customer success scores, 14 points higher
revenue retention, and 2.5x higher median MRR than customers who hadn't
customized at all. That attribution is correlational, not causal — customers
who customize more are likely more engaged overall — but it validated the
strategic bet that customization tooling is tied to business outcomes.

Reception during research was strong, and the framework's adoption by other
teams afterward was the strongest validation of the work: other product teams
built their own customization surfaces on top of the same infrastructure with
minimal oversight from ours, which wouldn't have happened if the underlying
patterns hadn't held up.

What's still unknown: the long-term impact on deal win rates where
customization gaps had previously been cited as a loss reason, and how far the
programmatic page editor vision — still early when my focus shifted to CRM
Index work — will ultimately extend across the product.

## Reflection

The content-first finding was the most consequential moment in this project.
If we'd skipped the research, or run it as a validation exercise instead of
genuine exploration, we'd have shipped a prettier version of the same
fragmented experience. The collaborative exercise — admins building their
ideal record page from a blank canvas — was the method that surfaced the
insight. Standard interview questions wouldn't have caught it, because admins
would have described their process using our existing vocabulary of sidebars,
tabs, and sections. A blank canvas forced them to reveal how they actually
think.

Constraining to the middle column first was the right call, even though it
frustrated customers who needed sidebar improvements sooner. It let us build
the foundation properly and get real feedback before scaling horizontally.

If I had it to do again, I'd fight harder to include activity feed relocation
in the first release. Research and CSAT data both supported it, and it stayed
one of the most visible limitations of the shipped product. I deferred it for
cross-team complexity; in hindsight, the coordination cost was worth paying
earlier, before the organizational context shifted and it became harder to
prioritize.

The platform outcome — the card builder framework, the programmatic editor
vision, adoption by other teams — wasn't the original mandate. I made
deliberate architectural choices knowing they'd have reuse value, but the
platform story emerged from those choices rather than being planned top-down
from the start. That's the lesson I carry forward: design for the immediate
problem, but make structural decisions that don't foreclose on the bigger
picture.
