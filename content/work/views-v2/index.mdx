HubSpot's CRM Index is the highest-traffic page in the product, and for years
its save behavior worked in ways nobody could reliably predict — not admins,
not reps, not the team that built it. Changes sometimes persisted. Sometimes
they didn't. Nobody could explain why. So users did the rational thing: they
stopped trusting shared views and started cloning everything. The result was
portals bloated with hundreds of duplicate views, admins unable to maintain
any source of truth, and reps working off stale data they didn't know was
stale. We'd been reading that cloning behavior as a preference for personal
workspaces. It wasn't — it was a coping mechanism. This project started with
that realization, and spent the better part of a year building the shared
language to actually ship the fix.

## My role

I own the CRM Index page end-to-end — records, views, and all six
visualization types: table, board, calendar, map, gantt, and report. On this
project I drove the end-to-end UX from problem framing through the solution
framework, cross-team alignment, and front-end design spec: the conceptual
model and internal framework docs, the sharing redesign, the three-tier roles
system, the base layer / working layer interaction model, and the
deviated-state indicator that surfaces divergence across the UI.

This was a cross-functional effort with contributions from an adjacent
pipelines team on early feature-audit scaffolding, and required alignment with
the board, calendar, gantt, and other visualization teams whose settings plug
into the views system.

## The problem

### User problem

The core failure was unpredictability. Users couldn't reliably answer the
question: if I change something on this view, what happens? Sometimes changes
saved. Sometimes they reverted on refresh. Sometimes they affected everyone on
the view, sometimes just the person making the change. The rules were
different for default views, shared custom views, and private views, and
nothing in the UI communicated those distinctions.

The downstream effects were extensive and well documented across NPS, CSAT,
our public ideas forum, and research. Admins reported their teams constantly
asking why views were reverting or behaving differently. Users described
other people's filters bleeding into views that weren't theirs. Teams lost
the ability to maintain a shared source of truth because only the original
creator could save — so everyone cloned their own copy instead.

<PullQuote attribution="CSAT feedback">
Views seem to change on themselves — filters from other users apply to your
views even though they can't or don't save them. It's just a mess.
</PullQuote>

The permission model compounded everything. It was binary — you created a
view, or you watched it — with no middle ground to say "this person can
adjust filters for their own workflow, but can't change what everyone else
sees." That gap forced the only workaround available: clone and diverge.

### Business problem

Views are one of the most consistent drivers of negative NPS and CSAT on the
Index. A company-wide quality initiative and a renewed focus on
smaller-customer CRM usage made this impossible to keep deferring. More
urgently, views aren't a CRM-only problem — the same architecture powers
workspaces, asset listing pages, and every other listing experience across
the product. Fixing it only for CRM and leaving the rest inconsistent would
have solved the symptom in one place while the underlying system stayed
broken everywhere else.

There was also a compounding org risk: admins who built views for their teams
could lose them entirely if the creator left the organization, since nobody
else had access to maintain them.

## Context and constraints

The highest-traffic page in the product cannot have surprises in a release.
That shaped every decision. Change aversion at scale — across customers
ranging from solo users to enterprise portals with thousands of views — meant
the solution had to preserve what people were already doing while making the
correct behavior the default.

We also inherited years of technical inconsistency: different save behaviors
for different view types, some of which customers had built workflows on top
of. Any new model had to degrade gracefully for those users rather than
breaking them.

The cross-team surface area created its own friction. Several visualization
teams each own their own settings, and getting them into a shared system
meant convincing those teams the framework was worth adopting — which meant
the framework had to be simple enough to implement without requiring anyone
to rebuild their entire settings architecture from scratch.

## Process

Before any solution work, I needed to understand what we were actually
dealing with. I started with a competitive deep-dive across several adjacent
productivity tools, specifically looking at how they handled the tension
between admin control and end-user flexibility. What I found was consistent
across all of them: a cascading permission model, a clear distinction between
view structure and working data, and some form of personal state persistence.
We had none of this. The more interesting finding was how our own model
compared to CRM peers, which were just as binary as we were — the gap between
what we offered and what the wider category expected had widened without
anyone noticing.

This sounds like it should have been obvious, but before we could design a
new system, we needed internal alignment on something we'd never formally
codified: what makes a view a view? What settings belong to its identity, and
which ones are just someone's working preferences? I ran a full audit of
every customizable setting across all six visualization types and categorized
each one along three axes — can an owner set it and save it to the view, can
a non-owner override it, and if they override it, does that change persist or
reset on refresh. The exercise produced dozens of line items and four clear
patterns: definitive view parameters that make up a view's identity (name,
permissions, visualization type, columns); flexible parameters owners set but
users can temporarily override (filters, sorting, pipeline); view preferences
that are personal and persist per view (filter bar visibility, collapsed
lanes); and global preferences that persist across every view (row height,
pagination).

The insight that came out of the exercise: save behavior wasn't confusing
because the rules were wrong. It was confusing because the rules differed
across these four categories, there was no UI signal about which category a
setting belonged to, and we'd never told anyone what the rules were.

With that taxonomy in hand, I worked through several iterations of a
conceptual framework to give the team shared vocabulary: view controls (what
can change), view roles (who can change it), and view layers (how changes
stack and interact). The layers model was the most important piece — a
template layer that instantiates new views and then disappears, a base layer
that's the single source of truth published by owners, and a working layer
that holds each user's personal deviations, auto-saved in perpetuity and
always resettable. The working layer was the core unlock: right now, filters
and sorting changes vanish on refresh, which is the source of most lost-work
complaints. Persisting it means you leave, you come back, and your work is
exactly where you left it — and when an owner publishes changes to the base
layer, users see a nudge that new changes are available, without losing their
current context.

Once the model was stable enough to share, I recorded a walkthrough
presenting the framework across every team consuming the views architecture,
laying out what we were building for CRM, the shared system we wanted
everyone to eventually adopt, and what it would take for their team to plug
in. This phase moved slower than the design work — teams had their own
roadmaps, and some had already made implementation decisions that needed
revisiting to fit the model. The framework docs existed specifically to make
those conversations tractable, giving everyone the same vocabulary before
getting into implementation details.

With the model aligned, I moved into detailed front-end design: the sharing
modal redesigned for granular role assignment and team-based access, role
state treatments across all six visualization types, the footer interaction
model for reset, clone, and publish actions visible to different roles in
different states, and the deviated-state indicator. That indicator was its
own design problem — early versions put a dot on filters, sorting, settings,
and the view tab, which created a page permanently speckled with indicators
since everyone is always in some deviated state. I pulled it back to a single
indicator on the view settings button, which groups all the deviated-state
actions in one place.

## Key decisions

### 1. Private-first defaults for all new views

**What was decided:** All newly created views default to private, regardless
of how they're created — sharing is an intentional act.

**Alternatives considered:** Keep the existing behavior, where a new view
inherits the visibility of the last view you created.

**Why this direction:** The existing default created accidental public views
constantly. Private-first matches the mental model of most modern
collaboration tools — start with yours, share when ready — and simplifies the
layer model, since private views don't need a distinction between base and
working layers.

**What it cost:** Some behavior change for power users who habitually created
shared views by default. Manageable through communication, not a blocker.

### 2. One primary visualization per view, table always available as secondary

**What was decided:** Each view is anchored to a single primary
visualization plus a table. Users can toggle between the two; free-switching
across all visualization types is gone.

**Alternatives considered:** Keep the existing behavior, where users can
switch freely among any visualization at any time.

**Why this direction:** Data showed the large majority of visualization
switching was between one visualization and the table, not across
visualization types — board-to-gantt or calendar-to-map switches were rare,
and became clone cases instead. Removing free-switching also eliminated a
category of technical complexity where a view could be in an ambiguous state
across multiple visualizations at once.

**What it cost:** Users who relied on multi-visualization flexibility within
a single view now need to clone — a reasonable workflow for a genuinely
different working context, but a real change to existing behavior.

### 3. Working layer persists in perpetuity

**What was decided:** Every user's working layer — their deviations from the
base layer — auto-saves and persists across sessions. Refreshing the page no
longer wipes filters or sorting.

**Alternatives considered:** Keep temporary state that wipes on refresh.
Introduce a manual save specifically for the working layer.

**Why this direction:** Temporary state was the primary driver of lost-work
complaints and directly responsible for defensive cloning. A manual save for
the working layer would have introduced a third save concept on top of
base-layer publish and working-layer reset — too much cognitive overhead.

**What it cost:** Persistence requires backend storage per user per view,
real engineering investment, and new edge cases — like what happens when
someone returns to a view a year later with a stale working context.
Addressed with a reset nudge whenever new base-layer changes have been
published.

### 4. Three roles, not two

**What was decided:** Owner, Collaborator, Member — replacing the existing
binary of creator-can-save versus everyone-else-can't.

**Alternatives considered:** An even simpler two-role model closer to
today's. A more granular model with field-level permissions. An earlier draft
with four roles, including a read-only "Visitor" role.

**Why this direction:** Two roles don't cover the real spread of use cases.
Admins need to give trusted teammates editing access without making them full
owners, and need to put less experienced users on a tighter leash without
full read-only. Three roles maps to three real archetypes: the person who
owns the view, the trusted power user who needs to work flexibly within it,
and the rep who should stay focused on records.

**What it cost:** More surface area for disabled states and tooltips across
every visualization type, and a permissions matrix that runs long. There are
still open edge cases, particularly around the Member role and filters.

## The solution

This work ships a redesigned system across three interconnected pieces. A
layer model, where every shared view has a base layer (the source of truth,
published by owners) and a working layer (each user's personal context,
auto-saved and persisted) — private views collapse the two into one, and users
can always reset back to the base layer.

## Outcomes

This work hasn't shipped to production yet, so there's no post-launch data to
report — CSAT and NPS movement, view-proliferation rate, working-layer
adoption, and reset-button usage are the metrics I plan to track once it
does.

What's already validated: research reception was strong going in, and the
underlying settings taxonomy and layer model have held up through review with
every team consuming the views architecture, which was the real test of
whether the framework was sound before writing a line of production code.

What's still unknown: how the working-layer persistence model behaves at
scale — stale working contexts for infrequent users, cross-team effects when
someone's working layer diverges significantly from a heavily updated base
layer — and how many teams beyond CRM adopt the shared framework once it
ships, which will be the real test of whether the system scales the way it
was designed to.

## Reflection

The thing I kept telling people throughout this project — in nearly every
walkthrough I recorded — was that the solution isn't that different from the
world we have today. I meant it as reassurance, so people wouldn't feel
overwhelmed by the framework. But it was a little disingenuous, because what
I actually built was less a new product experience and more a shared
conceptual system that had never existed before.

The settings taxonomy and the layer model aren't visible to customers. All of
it was internal scaffolding to get a large cross-functional group to agree on
what a view is before trying to redesign one. That work took longer than the
design work itself, and at times it felt like I was writing documentation for
a product that didn't exist yet instead of shipping something. In
retrospect, that was the right call — every time we tried to skip it and jump
to design, we hit alignment problems downstream that sent us back to
definitions.

What I'd do differently: I'd have pushed for a clearer scope gate earlier.
The version of this problem that spans every listing surface across the
product is a multi-year problem. The version that's just the CRM Index is a
much shorter one. We spent real time in the overlap between those two scope
levels before I made a firm call to keep the first phase CRM-only and let the
framework docs handle cross-team adoption separately. Making that call a
couple of months earlier would have focused the design work significantly.

The insight about cloning as a coping mechanism, not a preference, came
relatively late — after the competitive audit and the feature audit were both
complete. I wish I'd had that framing earlier; it would have sharpened the
problem statement from the start and made the case for working-layer
persistence easier to make to engineering. Instead we arrived at it through
the back door of a feature-level audit, which felt more like discovery than
design.
