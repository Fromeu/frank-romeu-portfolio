Staffing agents at Aquent were evaluating creative talent the same way
they'd evaluate anyone else: resumes, keyword searches, and gut instinct.
Portfolios — the single most valuable signal for creative roles, with 92% of
agents rating them more valuable than resumes — were reviewed in under 15
seconds and couldn't be searched at all. I led the design of a platform that
used machine learning to classify and index portfolio images, turning
visual work into a searchable, filterable recruitment tool. The harder
design problem wasn't the search. It was making individual portfolio images
tell enough of a story to be useful — which forced a fundamental shift in
what this product was trying to be.

## My role

I was the sole designer on Aquent's BOOK, responsible for the full product
design from research through release — the first time I designed an
application from the ground up. I owned UX research (survey design,
interview facilitation, persona creation), information architecture,
interaction design, visual design, prototyping, and usability testing. I
also collaborated with engineering on the machine learning search model,
helping curate and structure the image classification labels that powered
the search experience.

The team: one product manager, one agile development manager, five
developers, one data scientist, and two QA specialists. I was the only
designer.

## The problem

### User problem

Aquent's staffing agents had no visual search tool. When sourcing creative
talent, they relied on the same text-based tools and processes used for
non-creative roles — keyword searches through a database that couldn't
understand what a portfolio actually showed. Agents were reviewing
portfolios manually, spending 15 to 60 seconds per candidate, and they
could only get to a portfolio after already identifying a candidate through
other means. There was no way to start from the work itself and discover
talent through it.

Forty-two percent of respondents in my own research reported using external
visual search tools like Behance and Dribbble to supplement Aquent's own
systems. The sourcing workflow was split across tools that didn't talk to
each other, and the richest signal — the portfolio — was the hardest to
access at scale.

### Business problem

BOOK was one of Aquent's top strategic initiatives. The staffing industry
competes on speed and fit — the agency that surfaces the right candidate
fastest wins the placement. Aquent had a competitive asset (a database of
creative portfolios with years of accumulated work) but no way to
operationalize it. The platform was funded as a differentiator: proof that
Aquent was using technology to evolve creative sourcing beyond what
competitors could offer. Success would be measured in revenue from
placements, fill rate, and time to fill.

## Context and constraints

This was my first time designing a full application, not a feature within
an existing product. Every structural decision — information architecture,
interaction patterns, visual language — was mine to make without an
existing system to build on or reference. That freedom was also the
constraint: there were no guardrails.

A senior engineer had already built a machine learning proof of concept
that classified portfolio images before I joined. The POC secured funding
and set the technical foundation, but it also anchored the team's mental
model around "image search" as the core product concept — a framing my
research would eventually challenge.

The machine learning model operated on three pillars: image classification
labels (roughly 100 categories built from 1,000+ curated training images
each), object detection labels (3,000+ labels via Amazon's Rekognition
API), and natural language processing on resume and portfolio text. The
search experience had to be designed around what the model could reliably
classify, not around the ideal search vocabulary agents might want.

<Figure

  caption="The model's three pillars: classification labels, object detection, and text NLP."
/>

During interviews, agents struggled to articulate what search terms they'd
use for a visual search tool — not a failure of research, but a genuine
gap. Agents had never had this capability, so they couldn't easily
conceptualize it. The design had to bridge that gap rather than rely on
familiar search patterns.

## Process

The product's significance to Aquent's strategy earned proper research and
discovery time. I structured it in two phases: an internal phase
(stakeholder and engineering interviews on business operations, technical
considerations, and the state of the portfolio database), and a
domain-specific phase (auditing existing search tools, reviewing legacy
feedback, and studying design patterns from leading search platforms).

<Figure
  src="/work/book-talent-search/research-discovery.png"
  alt="Research discovery notes auditing existing search tools and design patterns"
  caption="Domain-specific discovery: auditing existing tools and legacy search feedback."

I designed and distributed a survey to 252 Aquent agents, recruiters, and
managers; 147 responded. The data confirmed the opportunity: 92% rated
portfolios as more valuable than resumes for assessing creative talent, 42%
were already using external visual tools, and 22% reviewed portfolios in
less than 15 seconds. Portfolios were the most valued signal and the least
supported by existing tools.

<Figure
  src="/work/book-talent-search/survey-results.png"
  alt="Survey results dashboard showing agent responses on portfolio value and search tool usage"
  caption="Survey data from 147 respondents: portfolios as the most valued, least supported signal."
/>

A product manager and I conducted 21 interviews with agents — she handled
logistics, I developed the script and facilitated. Key insights: agents
spent 15–60 seconds on a portfolio, portfolios were the first thing
reviewed once a candidate was identified, search terms were hard for agents
to conceptualize, clients articulated needs in relation to specific
projects or comparable talent rather than abstract skills, and singular
images rarely told the full story of a talent's fit. That last insight
would prove critical — it meant a results page full of individual images
wouldn't be enough; agents needed to see work in context.

I created a persona representing the staffing-agent audience and used it in
every brainstorming session. The research pointed to three core
experiences: a Search Results Page for searching and filtering, a Saved
Talent Page for refining selections and taking action, and a Project Detail
screen for viewing portfolio projects in context — accessible from both
other screens, because wherever an agent was in their workflow, they'd
eventually need to see the work in context.

I started with paper sketches, moved to mid-fidelity wireframes, then
assembled prototypes for testing. The initial direction drew heavily from
search-engine patterns — the research had started there, and the proof of
concept reinforced that mental model. But feedback revealed an opportunity
to differentiate: rather than building another search tool, we could
combine search capability with portfolio showcasing inspired by
creative-discovery platforms. The product's job shifted from "find images
by keyword" to "discover talent through their work." This wasn't a pivot
born of failure — it was recognizing that the most interesting thing about
BOOK wasn't the search, it was that the search gave you access to work you
could actually evaluate in context.

A few interaction decisions mattered more than the rest. A horizontal
masonry layout for search results, respecting the mostly-landscape
orientation of the portfolio images in the database. The mental model
"search for images, filter for talent" — search targeted the work, filters
targeted the person (location, pay rate, last active date) — validated
directly with users. And a full-screen Project View, grouping related
images and text into project views so agents could follow a portfolio's
actual narrative instead of evaluating a single disconnected image.

<PullQuote attribution="Research finding">
Singular images rarely tell the full story of a talent's fit.
</PullQuote>

Availability was the hardest filter to get right. Previous internal tools
had tried to standardize "availability" with varying degrees of success,
creating confusion and distrust in the data. Agents told me they trusted
calculated or implied data over self-reported data, and that they'd
ultimately verify availability through direct communication anyway. Instead
of designing around arbitrary availability dates, I built around concrete
activity dates: a binary toggle for working/non-working talent, filterable
statuses automated from reliable data sources, prioritized contact
information, and visible activity history — reframing the filter from "is
this person available?" (unreliable) to "when was this person last active?"
(verifiable).

Visual design worked within a color system Aquent's Product and Design team
had established in 2018 — balanced and brand-agnostic across subdivisions.
For BOOK, I used blue as the primary accent for talent-related components
(neutral, professional, calming) and green for action-commitment features
("green means go"). Typography was Roboto, chosen to echo the familiarity
of the Google Workspace tools the organization used universally.

## Key decisions

### 1. Pivoting from search-engine patterns to creative discovery

**What was decided:** Shift the product's core framing from a search tool
to a creative-discovery platform that happened to be searchable.

**Alternatives considered:** Stay with a more conventional search-engine
model, which would have been faster to build and easier to explain.

**Why this direction:** A conventional search tool would have competed
with existing platforms like Behance and Dribbble on their own terms,
without their network effects. Research and stakeholder feedback both
pointed to portfolio showcasing — not search mechanics — as the real
differentiator.

**What it cost:** Redesigning significant portions of the interaction
model mid-project, and resetting stakeholder expectations around what the
product would look and feel like.

### 2. Activity-based filtering over availability dates

**What was decided:** Replace a self-reported "availability" concept with
a filter built on verifiable, system-derived activity dates.

**Alternatives considered:** Another attempt at a reliable self-reported
availability signal.

**Why this direction:** Previous tools had tried this and failed — agents
didn't trust the data. Reframing around "last active" dates derived from
real system data was honest about what the system could actually know.

**What it cost:** Agents still had to contact talent directly to confirm
true availability. But they were doing that anyway; the design acknowledged
reality instead of trying to engineer around it.

### 3. Full-screen project view over inline image detail

**What was decided:** Replace an inline portfolio preview with a
full-screen Project View.

**Alternatives considered:** Enrich the inline preview with more data
while keeping it lightweight.

**Why this direction:** Research showed individual images weren't enough
to evaluate fit — agents needed the narrative context of a full project:
multiple images, sequencing, and text together.

**What it cost:** A more interruptive flow when browsing results — each
click became a bigger commitment. But the quality of information at that
point was dramatically higher.

## Outcomes

Three months post-release (September 2019): $7.4M in revenue from roles
placed through BOOK, an 8% improvement in fill rate, a 12% reduction in
average time to fill, and 98.7% retention for roles placed through the
platform. Attribution is direct — these are roles placed specifically
through BOOK — but three months is a short window for a platform still
being adopted internally.

Agents had, for the first time, a visual-first entry point to their
sourcing process. The platform was featured in Aquent's sales conversations
with clients as a differentiator. The ML model's classification accuracy
was sufficient for useful search results, though the curated label set —
roughly 100 categories — represented only a fraction of how agents might
naturally describe creative work.

What's still unknown: how fill rate and time-to-fill held up as adoption
matured, how the classification model would need to evolve to match the
full vocabulary agents actually use, and whether the platform reduced
reliance on external tools like Behance and Dribbble or simply added
another tool to the stack.

## Reflection

This project taught me more about product design than anything I'd done
before it — not because of the machine learning component, but because
designing from scratch forced me to own every structural decision without
the scaffolding of an existing product.

What worked: the research investment. The survey and 21 interviews gave us
signal that directly shaped the product — the activity-over-availability
insight, the "singular images don't tell the story" finding, the "search
for images, filter for talent" mental model. None of that came from
competitive analysis or assumption; it came from agents describing their
actual work.

What didn't work as well: the initial direction. Starting from
search-engine patterns was the obvious move, but it anchored the team — and
me — on a paradigm that didn't serve the product's real opportunity. We
corrected course, but the pivot cost time and required resetting
expectations. If I'd spent more of the early research phase asking "what
makes this different from Behance?" instead of "how do search engines
work?", we might have arrived at the creative-discovery framing sooner.

The availability problem also stayed messier than I wanted. The
activity-based reframe was honest — it acknowledged that availability data
is inherently unreliable — but agents still had to pick up the phone. I
don't know if that's fully solvable with data, and I left it as an open
question rather than pretending the design resolved it.

If I had a clean slate, I'd push harder on the Project View concept
earlier. It ended up being the most differentiated part of the product —
the thing that made BOOK more than "Dribbble but internal." Getting there
faster would have given us more time to refine it.
