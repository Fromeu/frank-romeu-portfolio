Every app in HubSpot's ecosystem had its own idea of what a settings page
should look like — and most of them were wrong in different ways.
HubSpot-built integrations were wildly inconsistent; externally-built apps
had no settings pages at all. Twenty-seven percent of integration support
calls were driven by settings confusion alone. I led the design of a
framework to fix that — not just for our own apps, but eventually for every
developer building on the platform.

## My role

I was the sole product designer on this project across both phases
(2020–2022), embedded in HubSpot's Developer Product Group. I owned the
end-to-end design: research planning and facilitation, information
architecture, UI design from wireframes through high-fidelity, usability
testing, and the design of the UI Builder tool that made the framework
publicly available. I also facilitated a card sort study and partner
research interviews, and drove the pitch to return to the project after a
year away.

I partnered closely with a product manager, two tech leads (front-end and
back-end), a content designer, and a product analyst. Six engineers built
it.

## The problem

### User problem

Administrators managing HubSpot integrations were spending disproportionate
time on configuration tasks that should have been straightforward. Settings
pages across native apps had no structural consistency — Salesforce settings
looked nothing like QuickBooks, which looked nothing like Shopify. The
cognitive load of relearning each app's layout compounded across the
nine-plus integrations a typical admin might touch. Twenty-seven percent of
integration support calls were specifically about settings. Fifty-seven
percent of total integration support volume was driven by the Salesforce app
alone — the oldest and largest integration in the ecosystem.

<PullQuote attribution="Customer feedback">
I primarily live in Salesforce and so does my data. It's increasingly
painful to use HubSpot because the process of syncing my SF-data is
absolutely horrendous. I shouldn't have to spend hours in HubSpot just to
understand my syncs.
</PullQuote>

### Business problem

HubSpot's Developer Product Group existed to give external developers the
same ability to create quality experiences as internal teams. Settings was a
clear failure of that promise: HubSpot-built integrations had inconsistent
but existing settings; externally-built apps had none. This wasn't just a UX
problem — it undermined the platform's credibility with the App Partner
ecosystem and limited the quality ceiling for the entire marketplace. The
inconsistency also created a growing support cost that scaled with every new
integration added.

## Context and constraints

App settings were owned by individual app teams, not a centralized platform
team. Each team had extended the settings experience to fit their specific
needs, which meant the framework had to accommodate genuine variation
without enabling further fragmentation. I was designing a standard for teams
I didn't manage.

Phase 1 shipped in mid-2020, then the project paused to collect metrics and
the team moved to other priorities. When I pitched Phase 2 a year later, the
framework had been adopted — and modified — by teams building Operations
Hub, Zoom, MS Teams, and Slack integrations, all without centralized design
coordination. I returned to a framework that had drifted.

The "Data sync" portion of the framework was omitted from the public release
due to technical complexity — a real constraint that limited what external
developers could build, meaning the public framework was a subset of what
native apps used.

This was effectively a mini component library for a specific surface, not
backed by a broader design system team. Managing it across multiple
designers and teams over time was my responsibility alongside the feature
design work.

## Process

The inconsistency of app settings was something the team acknowledged
informally, but I wanted quantitative grounding before committing to a
solution direction. I partnered with a product analyst to pull data from our
analytics and support-ticket systems. The numbers confirmed what we
suspected: 27% of integration support calls revolved around settings, 63%
around data syncs and related tasks, and a single integration — Salesforce —
drove 57% of total support volume. These weren't edge cases; settings
confusion was a systemic problem scaling with the ecosystem.

With the problem validated, I audited settings functionality across nine
native integrations: QuickBooks Online, Salesforce, Sansan, Shopify, Zoom,
Slack, GoToWebinar, Microsoft Teams, and Google Contacts. The audit surfaced
a consistent set of user needs beneath the surface-level inconsistency:
managing connected accounts, toggling syncs per object type, adjusting
create/update/delete rules, mapping properties between systems, and learning
about app features. These became the structural pillars of the framework —
an information architecture organized around four segments: Account
details, Sync health, Object sync, and Getting started.

I moved from low-fidelity wireframes to high-fidelity mockups, running
moderated and unmoderated usability tests with HubSpot customers before
shipping. The framework launched in July 2020 for five native integrations.
We set success targets — cumulative NPS of 25+, a 15% reduction in
Salesforce support volume, 50% of native apps on the framework — then paused
to let the data accumulate.

A year later, I pitched returning to the framework. The landscape had
shifted: it had become the foundation for settings across 100+ data sync
apps in Operations Hub, and other teams had extended it with new apps, each
pulling it in a slightly different direction. Before I could design the
public version, I had to take stock of what the framework had become and
reconcile the divergent implementations.

A product manager and I conducted 12 exploratory interviews with App
Partners — external developers building apps for the HubSpot Marketplace.
She handled outreach and scheduling; I drove the research plan and
facilitated the calls. Two primary needs emerged: the ability to trigger
actions in external systems from HubSpot, and better onboarding to help
users get value from newly installed apps.

I ran an open card sort with 24 HubSpot customers to understand how they
spatially organized the feature set — deliberately open-ended, no pre-set
categories, for unbiased signal on their actual mental models. The results
informed a revised information architecture: four top-level tabs (Account
settings, General settings, Data sync, Feature discovery) with consistency
at the top level and room for customization in sub-tabs beneath.

Making the framework available to external developers required a
configuration tool. I coordinated with both tech leads to scope what became
the App Settings UI Builder — accessible from HubSpot Developer accounts,
letting App Partners configure settings pages through three component
types: an Account component, Control components (buttons, toggles,
dropdowns), and Feature cards. It included a live preview, manual and
auto-save, template settings, and version history.

I conducted research calls with 8 App Partners using an assembled prototype
to validate the updated framework and assess UI Builder usability. The
calls confirmed our direction and surfaced refinements: an improved preview
window, template settings, dropdown support in control components, version
history, and a guided onboarding flow.

## Key decisions

### 1. Pitching Phase 2 after a year away

**What was decided:** Advocate for returning to the project once the
team's roadmap had moved on, rather than letting the framework remain
internal-only.

**Alternatives considered:** Leave the framework as an internal-only tool
and let the distributed drift continue unchecked.

**Why this direction:** The framework had hit its Phase 1 goals, but the
original vision — available to every developer — was incomplete. Leaving it
internal-only would have left the platform promise unfulfilled.

**What it cost:** Real re-scoping work. A year of organic growth had
changed what "the framework" actually meant, and I had to reconcile that
before designing forward.

### 2. Open card sort over closed

**What was decided:** Run the Phase 2 information-architecture research as
an open card sort — no pre-set categories.

**Alternatives considered:** A closed card sort, faster to analyze and more
directly actionable.

**Why this direction:** A closed sort would have anchored customers to our
existing structure. The open format revealed how customers naturally
grouped features, which didn't always match our internal model.

**What it cost:** A messier synthesis process — but the signal was more
honest.

### 3. Omitting Data Sync from the public framework

**What was decided:** Scope the public framework to exclude the Data Sync
component, due to technical complexity.

**Alternatives considered:** A longer timeline to ship the full framework
publicly, Data Sync included.

**Why this direction:** Shipping what was ready let external developers
build account management, controls, and feature discovery immediately,
while native apps retained the full feature set.

**What it cost:** A real capability gap between first-party and
third-party apps, which partially undercut the framework's "level playing
field" premise.

### 4. Revised IA with flexible sub-tabs

**What was decided:** Consistent top-level tabs with customizable sub-tabs
beneath them, replacing a more rigid original structure.

**Alternatives considered:** Enforcing a single, identical structure for
every app.

**Why this direction:** The flexible approach accommodated genuine
variation across apps while maintaining the structural coherence admins
needed to move between them.

**What it cost:** More complexity for developers configuring their
settings — more choices to make, more ways to make them poorly.

## The solution

The shipped product has two faces. For HubSpot admins: a standardized
settings experience across native integrations, organized around account
management, general controls, per-object data sync configuration, and
feature discovery — consistent navigation and structure regardless of which
app they're configuring.

## Outcomes

Fifteen native apps adopted the framework, with an average NPS of 87 across
them — well past the original target of 25+. Support volume from the
Salesforce integration dropped, though the exact figure in the underlying
data is inconsistent enough that I'd want to verify it precisely before
citing it — the honest version is that it moved in the right direction,
short of the 15% target we'd originally set.

The framework became the default settings foundation for Operations Hub's
100+ data sync apps — adoption well beyond the original scope. Multiple
design teams across HubSpot adopted the framework independently, which
validated the structural model but also revealed the distributed-ownership
problem Phase 2 had to address.

What's still unknown: long-term support-volume trends since Phase 2, how
NPS trends for third-party apps built on the still-beta UI Builder, and
whether omitting Data Sync from the public version creates meaningful
friction for App Partners over time.

## Reflection

The framework worked. The structure held across native apps, earned strong
NPS scores, and scaled beyond its original scope into Operations Hub. The
UI Builder gave external developers a real path to building settings pages
that didn't exist before.

What worked specifically: the open card sort in Phase 2. Running it
open-ended rather than closed revealed that customers didn't organize
features the way we expected, which led to a better IA. The App Partner
research was also critical — their needs (action triggers, onboarding) were
different from what I'd assumed based on internal usage patterns.

What didn't work: the start/stop cadence. Pausing for a year was justified
— we needed the data — but the distributed ownership model meant other
teams extended the framework without coordinated design guidance in the
meantime. When I returned, I spent real time reconciling divergent
implementations before I could design forward, which wasn't in the original
plan and compressed the Phase 2 timeline.

If I had a clean slate, I'd build lightweight governance into the framework
from the start — design guidelines, review touchpoints, a shared library
with guardrails — so distributed teams extending it would stay within
bounds even without centralized oversight. The technical framework was
sound; the organizational framework around it was what drifted. That's a
distinction I now think about on every systems-level project: a feature
serves users directly, but a framework serves the teams who serve users,
and its design constraints are organizational as much as functional.
